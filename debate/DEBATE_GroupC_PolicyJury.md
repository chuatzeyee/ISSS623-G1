# Group C - Policy Jury: Evaluation Criteria & Balanced Synthesis

**Your role is explicitly NOT to just pick a winner.** You must (1) evaluate strengths, weaknesses, risks, and policy implications of BOTH framings, (2) say which side argued more persuasively and why, and (3) propose a balanced policy narrative for Singapore - "realistic, without being alarmist or overly optimistic." The exercise sheet states: *a strong synthesis recognises that both framings are partially valid but incomplete.*

Timeline: you get 5 min to prepare criteria while A/B huddle, then you listen (take structured notes), then 5 min to synthesize, then 4 min to present.

## Phase 2 - Pre-analyzed strengths & blind spots (so your 5-min huddle is confirmation, not creation)

### Framing 1: Ageing as healthcare & economic challenge

**Strengths (acknowledge these even if B spoke better):**
- Matches genuinely hard constraints: health spend $22B→$59B; workforce 129k→156k needed against a global 18M shortfall; 7–10-year hospital lead times. These are real and non-negotiable.
- Mobilizes urgency and budget; historically effective in SG (GST rise, CareShield, retirement-age moves were all won on this framing).
- Support ratio (5.7→2.7) is simple, projectable, comparable - a usable planning number.
- Pessimistic capacity planning is the rational asymmetric bet (under-building is irreversible; over-building is merely costly).

**Blind spots / risks:**
- **Ageist spillover:** system-level "burden" language leaks into public attitudes and senior self-perception; negative age perception is itself associated with worse health outcomes (Levy: ~7.5-year longevity gap) - the framing can worsen the very costs it forecasts.
- **Homogenizes the cohort:** treats all 65+ as dependent; most 65–74s are healthy and contributing.
- **Ignores uncounted contribution:** informal caregiving, grandparent childcare, volunteering - real fiscal substitution invisible to GDP and to the dependency ratio.
- **Measurement bias:** counts costs (bed-days, spending) but not assets (healthy life expectancy, participation), so policy optimizes for managing decline rather than reducing it.
- Alarmist vocabulary ("silver tsunami") mislabels a slow, predictable, success-driven transition as a disaster.

### Framing 2: Ageing as social resource & longevity opportunity

**Strengths:**
- Restores dignity and agency; counters self-fulfilling decline; aligns with evidence that perception affects health.
- Unlocks a wider policy menu: life-course redesign, reskilling, phased retirement, age-friendly cities, intergenerational housing (Kampung Admiralty), social prescribing - many of which are cheaper than the acute care they avert.
- Prevention logic is fiscally sound: compressing morbidity defers the most expensive care years; consistent with Healthier SG.
- Measures what you want to grow (healthy life expectancy, contribution), not only what you fear.

**Blind spots / risks:**
- **Can understate hard arithmetic:** optimism does not staff wards or build nursing homes; capacity gaps are irreversible on relevant timescales.
- **Complacency risk:** "opportunity" rhetoric can politically justify under-investment ("seniors are assets, so we need fewer beds").
- **Overgeneralizes from the healthy majority:** the frail minority with dementia and multimorbidity have genuinely heavy, costly needs; a strategy must plan for the tail, not the mean.
- Prevention pays back slowly and partially; even perfect compression of morbidity leaves a larger absolute number of very old people needing care.
- Contribution claims are hard to budget: informal care is real but cannot be redeployed by fiat.

## Phase 4 - The balanced synthesis to propose (your 4-min centerpiece)

**Headline narrative (say it as a slogan):**
> **"Plan for the costs. Invest in the capabilities. Retire the tsunami."**

Recommend the Ministry adopt a **dual-track framing - "a longevity transition to be managed"**:

1. **Challenge lens where it belongs - capacity & financing (the annexes and budgets).**
   Pessimistic planning for hospitals, ILTC, workforce (156k by 2030), and financing reform. Keep the support ratio as a planning indicator. Rationale: lead times are long and under-provision is irreversible - A's strongest ground.

2. **Asset lens where it belongs - society, work, and community (the narrative and national goals).**
   Life-course redesign, senior employment and reskilling, caregiver support, age-friendly and intergenerational design, Healthier SG-style prevention. Rationale: perception shapes behaviour and health; contribution is real fiscal substitution - B's strongest ground.

3. **Fix the measurement so both lenses stay honest.**
   Report **healthy life expectancy alongside the old-age support ratio**; track senior participation and informal-care contribution, not only cost per senior. What gets measured gets managed.

4. **Fix the language.**
   Strategy document uses "longevity society / super-aged transition"; drops "silver tsunami" and "burden." Costs are stated plainly as numbers - numbers inform without insulting.

5. **The two frames are complements, not rivals:** the asset agenda (prevention, participation) is the best long-run *cost-control strategy*, and the challenge agenda (funded capacity) is what makes the opportunity *credible*. Alarmism without investment breeds fatalism; optimism without capacity breeds crisis.

**Closing line:**
> "Singapore should write a strategy document that budgets like a pessimist and speaks like a builder. Both sides today were partially right and incomplete alone - realism about costs plus ambition about capability is the only framing that is neither alarmist nor naive."

**Optional course tie-in (strong finish for participation marks):**
> "And for this class: the framing decides the analytics. A challenge frame builds bed-demand and cost-prediction models; an asset frame builds healthy-ageing and participation analytics. A learning health system needs both."
