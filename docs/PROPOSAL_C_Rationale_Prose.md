# Rationale for the Caregiver-Focused Design and Construction of the Analytic Sample

*Proposal-ready prose for the group project document (Topic C, caregiver angle). Drop into the proposal body or an annex titled "Data feasibility and rationale".*

---

Our project question centres on caregiver status: whether providing regular care or assistance to a family member or friend is associated with missing, versus attending, one's own routine annual check-up. The focal variable, CAREGIV1 ("During the past 30 days, did you provide regular care or assistance to a friend or family member who has a health problem or disability?"), sits in the BRFSS Caregiver optional module, which was fielded by only 16 of 53 states and territories in 2024. Because it is an optional-module item, CAREGIV1 is not included in the course-supplied Topic C extract. We therefore extracted it ourselves from the raw fixed-width ASCII file (LLCP2024.ASC, 922 MB), following the extraction logic provided in the course's BRFSS_Extraction notebook: the variable dictionary supplies the column positions (CAREGIV1 occupies column 325), the caregiver fields were read with pandas' fixed-width reader, and the resulting columns were merged onto the supplied Topic C extract. Before merging, we verified row alignment by confirming that the state identifier (_STATE) matched exactly, row for row, across all 457,670 records in both files. This step responds directly to the Project Guidance slide's invitation to "go beyond these suggestions", and the merged working dataset is fully reproducible from the raw file and our notebook.

The recut of the data proceeds in three documented steps. Of the 457,670 respondents in the full 2024 file, 96,287 (21.0%) reside in the 16 states that fielded the Caregiver module and were therefore asked CAREGIV1; the remaining 79.0% are structurally missing - never asked - rather than non-respondents, and our missingness table reports this structural non-participation separately from item-level missingness. Of those asked, 94,670 have both a valid caregiver status and a valid check-up outcome. Applying the same per-variable missingness rules as the rest of our design yields a final analytic sample of 87,098 complete cases, a within-module retention rate of 92.0%. Two checks support the validity of this construction: the caregiver prevalence in our sample is 22.2%, consistent with the CDC's published estimate that roughly one in five US adults is an informal caregiver, and the outcome event rate in the final sample is 18.2%, essentially identical to the rate in the full national file - indicating that restricting to module states did not materially distort the outcome distribution.

The trade-off of this design is stated plainly. The analytic sample falls from approximately 382,000 respondents (in a generic all-states design) to 87,098, and our findings are generalisable only to the 16 module states rather than to all participating states. We accept this trade-off for three reasons. First, statistical power is not a concern: with roughly 15,800 outcome events across 19 predictors, we retain more than 4,000 events per predictor, far beyond conventional adequacy thresholds. Second, the sample remains large enough for stable train/test evaluation of all three planned models. Third, the restriction is the unavoidable price of studying a caregiver exposure at all - and the project brief itself cautions that optional modules "vary by state and should be used with caution"; our design demonstrates precisely that caution by naming the participating states, quantifying coverage, and scoping all claims to the module-state analytic sample in an extended limitation statement.

The scientific motivation for accepting this trade-off is a genuine empirical puzzle. The caregiver-health literature has long warned that caregivers postpone their own care while caring for others. Our bivariate check of the 2024 data shows the opposite: caregivers have a lower check-up gap rate than non-caregivers (15.0% versus 19.1%), and the difference persists within age bands (21.1% versus 26.7% among respondents under 65; 7.0% versus 7.4% among those 65 and over). However, caregivers in the module states skew female (60.0%) and slightly older - exactly the demographic profile associated with higher baseline check-up attendance - so the unadjusted comparison cannot be taken at face value. Whether the caregiver "advantage" survives adjustment for age, sex, health status, socioeconomic position and healthcare access is therefore the headline question of our analysis, and either answer is interpretable: persistence after adjustment would support a connection hypothesis (caregivers are already engaged with the health system through their care recipient's appointments), while attenuation or reversal would recover the neglect hypothesis for identifiable subgroups. We deliberately do not commit to a direction.

Two coding decisions specific to the caregiver variables merit documentation. First, in CAREGIV1 the response code 8 does not mean "none" - as 88 does in the day-count variables - but "expects to provide care to a family member or friend within the next two years" (n = 215). We recode 1 to caregiver, 2 to non-caregiver, and codes 7, 8 and 9 to missing, and note the decision in our variable dictionary; the same digit carries different meanings in different items, and handling each per the codebook is exactly the kind of recoding awareness the assessment rewards. Second, the caregiving-intensity items (CRGVHRS2 hours of care, CRGVREL5 relationship to the care recipient, CRGVLNG2 duration of caregiving) are asked only of respondents who answered yes to CAREGIV1. Including them as model predictors would perfectly leak caregiver status into the model; they are therefore used exclusively in a descriptive profile of the caregiver subgroup and never enter any predictive model.

Relative to a generic preventive-care-gap design, our predictor set changes in two ways while remaining one slot under the 20-variable cap, at 19 predictors. CAREGIV1 is added as the focal predictor, and _URBSTAT (urban/rural residence) is removed: the 16-state module footprint already constrains the geographic composition of the sample, so residual urban/rural variation is better acknowledged as a limitation than modelled as a predictor. Finally, the caregiver angle strengthens our literature anchoring: Schulz and Beach (1999, JAMA) established caregiving as a mortality risk factor, Bevans and Sternberg (2012, JAMA) reviewed the health effects of family caregiving, and Edwards et al. (2020, MMWR) profiled informal caregivers using this very BRFSS Caregiver module - giving our exposure, our dataset and our adjustment strategy (notably the inclusion of mental distress, which Edwards showed is elevated among caregivers) direct precedents in the published literature.
