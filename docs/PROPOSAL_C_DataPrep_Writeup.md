# Data Preparation Write-Up (Corrected and Extended)

**ISSS623 Group Project - Topic C (caregiver angle) · Reworded from the team draft; corrections noted at the end.**

---

We define "sought care" as having attended a routine check-up within the past year, derived from the key variable CHECKUP1, which asks: "About how long has it been since you last visited a doctor for a routine checkup?" We selected this as our outcome because it is the only utilisation measure in the 2024 BRFSS that is asked of all respondents regardless of age, sex or medical condition, and it is nearly complete, with only 1.1% invalid responses in our sample.

Using CAREGIV1 as our focal variable carries important constraints that we identified and addressed during data preparation. First, CAREGIV1 ("During the past 30 days, did you provide regular care or assistance to a friend or family member who has a health problem or disability?") belongs to the Caregiver optional module, which only 16 of 53 states and territories fielded in 2024; respondents in the remaining states were never asked the question, so their records are structurally missing rather than non-responses, and our missingness table reports the two separately. Second, because it is an optional-module item, CAREGIV1 is not included in the course-supplied Topic C extract. We therefore extracted it ourselves from the raw fixed-width ASCII file by adapting the course's BRFSS_Extraction notebook, reading CAREGIV1 (column 325), CRGVREL5 (columns 326-327), CRGVHRS2 (column 334) and CRGVLNG2 (column 335) at the positions given in the variable dictionary. Before merging these columns onto the Topic C extract to form our working dataset, we verified row alignment by confirming that the state identifier (_STATE) matched exactly, row for row, across all 457,670 records in both files. At this stage we also recoded the working dataset, including the CAREGIV1-specific trap that code 8 means "expects to provide care within two years" rather than "none" (see Annex 8.2 for the full recode rules and dictionary). The entire preparation step is reproducible from our notebook.

Our working dataset has 41 columns. Of the 457,670 respondents in the full 2024 file, 96,287 (21.0%) reside in the 16 module states and were asked the caregiver question; of these, 94,670 have both a valid caregiver status and a valid check-up outcome. Applying consistent per-variable missingness rules (Annex 8.3), we arrive at a final analytic sample of 87,098 complete cases - a within-module retention rate of 92.0%. Two checks support the validity of this construction. First, caregivers make up 22.2% of our analytic sample, consistent with the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's published estimate that roughly one in five US adults is an informal caregiver - a figure we would be unlikely to reproduce had the extraction's column offsets been wrong. Second, the outcome event rate in our final sample is 18.2%, essentially identical to the rate in the full national file, indicating that restricting the analysis to the 16 fielding states did not materially distort the outcome distribution.

Within this analytic sample, a further nested subset governs the caregiver-profile portion of our analysis. The three caregiver-detail items (relationship to the care recipient, hours of care per week, and duration of caregiving) are follow-up questions administered only to respondents who first answered "yes" to CAREGIV1. They therefore exist only for the 21,137 caregivers in the module sample: 20,976 of these have a valid check-up outcome, and applying the same per-variable missingness rules as the main analysis yields 19,263 caregiver complete cases, containing approximately 2,890 check-up-gap events (15.0%). Two design consequences follow. First, because the detail items' skip-logic missingness pattern is a perfect copy of caregiver status itself, including them as predictors in the main 87,098-case models would leak the focal variable - a model could reconstruct CAREGIV1 from the mere presence or absence of a value. They are therefore excluded from all predictive models and used exclusively in a descriptive profile of the caregiver subgroup (for example, gap rates by hours-of-care band and by relationship to the care recipient), where among caregivers they are 98.5-99.9% complete. Second, this ~19,000-case subset remains comfortably adequate for descriptive comparison - roughly 150 outcome events per predictor if it were ever modelled - but any caregivers-only predictive model would constitute a third modelling exercise beyond our scoped primary and secondary analyses, and is accordingly designated as optional exploratory work rather than a committed deliverable.

## Corrections made to the original team draft

1. **CHECKUP1 question text**: the draft quoted the CAREGIV1 question ("did you provide regular care or assistance...") under CHECKUP1. CHECKUP1 asks about time since the last routine check-up. Corrected.
2. **CDC 1-in-5 check re-anchored**: the draft attached the CDC caregiver-prevalence estimate to the 92% retention rate. It validates the 22.2% caregiver share of the sample (a number the draft omitted). Corrected and the prevalence figure added.
3. **Outcome-distribution claim given its own evidence**: "restriction did not distort the outcome" is supported by the 18.2% event rate matching the full national file - not by caregiver prevalence. Separated into two distinct validity checks.
4. **Terminology**: respondents do not "field" a module (states field it; respondents are asked); "derive at" corrected to "arrive at"; CDC spelled per its official name (Centers, not Centres, when naming the US agency).
5. **Added**: the caregivers-only nested subset paragraph (21,137 → 20,976 → 19,263, ~2,890 events), the leakage rationale for quarantining the detail items, and the scope status of any caregivers-only model.
