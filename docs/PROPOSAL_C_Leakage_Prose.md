# Prevention of Data Leakage and Separation of Analytic Roles

**ISSS623 Group Project - Topic C (caregiver angle) · Companion to `PROPOSAL_C_DataPrep_Methodology.md` (Section 7 in prose form)**
*Submission-ready prose for the proposal document or final report methodology section.*

---

Data leakage - the contamination of a predictive model by information that would not legitimately be available at prediction time, or by information from the evaluation data - is the most common way a predictive modelling exercise silently invalidates itself. Our design guards against it through four explicit mechanisms.

First, the caregiver-intensity items are quarantined from all predictive models. The variables CRGVHRS2 (hours of care provided per week), CRGVREL5 (relationship of the care recipient to the respondent) and CRGVLNG2 (duration of the caregiving episode) are follow-up questions administered only to respondents who first answered "yes" to CAREGIV1. Their missingness pattern is therefore not random noise but a perfect logical copy of the focal predictor itself: any respondent with a valid value on any of these items is, by construction, a caregiver. A model given access to them would not need to learn anything about caregiving and check-up behaviour - it could trivially reconstruct caregiver status from the mere presence or absence of an intensity value, and any apparent "importance" of these variables would be an artefact of the questionnaire's skip logic rather than a substantive finding. For this reason the three intensity items never enter any predictor set. They are used exclusively in a descriptive profile of the caregiver subgroup (for example, the distribution of weekly caregiving hours among the 21,137 caregivers), where they add interpretive colour without touching the models.

Second, the dual role of MEDCOST1 is cleanly separated across two independent analyses. MEDCOST1 (a cost-related barrier to seeing a doctor in the past 12 months) serves as a predictor in our primary model, where the target is the check-up gap derived from CHECKUP1: affordability is a core access mechanism and belongs in that model. In our secondary analysis, MEDCOST1 itself becomes the target, and at that point it is removed from the predictor set entirely - a variable can never appear on both sides of the same model. The two analyses share the cohort-construction pipeline and predictor framework but are otherwise independent model fits, each with its own outcome-specific exclusions (each analysis drops only the rows with an invalid value on its own target). This is stated explicitly to pre-empt the reasonable objection that one variable appearing in two roles across the project reflects confusion; it reflects two separate research questions answered with a shared, documented data infrastructure.

Third, no target-derived information enters any predictor set. No recode, transformation, interaction or derived flag computed from CHECKUP1 is available to the primary model, and likewise nothing derived from MEDCOST1 is available to the secondary model. This extends to subtler forms of the problem: we do not, for example, construct a "healthcare engagement" composite that would fold the outcome into a predictor under another name. Each variable in the dictionary has exactly one declared role - outcome, predictor, or descriptive-only - fixed before modelling begins, and the notebook enforces the predictor list programmatically so that a column cannot drift between roles across the team's work.

Fourth, the train/test split precedes every fitted decision. After cohort construction produces the 87,098-respondent analytic sample, we perform a single stratified 70/30 train/test split, stratified on the outcome so both partitions preserve the 18.2% event rate, with a fixed random seed so the split is identical for every model and reproducible by any team member or marker. All model fitting, hyperparameter choices (tree depth, minimum leaf size, number of trees), class-weight settings, threshold selection and descriptive-informed refinements are confined to the training partition. An important nuance is why our recoding can safely happen before the split: every recode in our variable dictionary is codebook-driven - a fixed mapping determined by the CDC's published value labels (7/9 to missing, 88 to zero days, insurance codes to named groups) - and none is estimated from the data. Codebook-driven transformations carry no information from any observation to any other, so applying them to the full cohort cannot leak test-set information into training. Had we instead used data-estimated preprocessing (for example, mean imputation, standardisation, or supervised binning), those parameters would have to be learned on the training partition only and then applied to the test partition; our design avoids this class of risk entirely by using no data-estimated preprocessing. Finally, the held-out test set is evaluated exactly once, at the end, to populate the final model-comparison table (accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, AUROC, AUPRC for the logistic baseline, decision tree and random forest, alongside a majority-class dummy). It is never used for model selection, threshold tuning or iterative improvement - repeated evaluation against a test set converts it, gradually and invisibly, into a second training set, and the single-evaluation rule is what preserves the comparison table's status as an honest estimate of out-of-sample performance.
